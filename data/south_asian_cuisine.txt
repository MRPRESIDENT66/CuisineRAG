BIRYANI — THE CROWN JEWEL OF SOUTH ASIAN CUISINE
Biryani is a fragrant, layered rice dish that originated in Persia and was brought to the Indian subcontinent by Mughal emperors.
It is made with long-grain basmati rice, meat (chicken, lamb, or beef), or vegetables, cooked with whole spices like cardamom,
cloves, cinnamon, bay leaves, and star anise. The dish is distinguished by its dum (slow-cooking) technique where rice and meat
are layered and sealed with dough or foil to trap steam. Saffron dissolved in warm milk is drizzled on top for color and aroma.
Popular regional varieties include Hyderabadi Biryani (India), Karachi Biryani (Pakistan), and Sindhi Biryani.
Hyderabadi Biryani uses raw meat layered with parboiled rice. Lucknowi (Awadhi) Biryani uses pre-cooked meat.
Biryani is typically served with raita (yogurt sauce), mirchi ka salan (chili curry), and salad.

BUTTER CHICKEN (MURGH MAKHANI)
Butter Chicken, known as Murgh Makhani, was invented in the 1950s at Moti Mahal restaurant in Delhi, India by Kundan Lal Gujral.
The dish features tandoor-cooked chicken simmered in a rich, creamy tomato-based sauce made with butter, cream, cashews,
and a blend of spices including fenugreek leaves (kasuri methi), garam masala, cumin, and coriander.
The sauce has a characteristic orange-red color and a mild, slightly sweet flavor profile.
The chicken is first marinated in yogurt and spices, then cooked in a tandoor (clay oven) or pan-fried.
Kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves) is the key ingredient that gives butter chicken its distinctive taste.
It is best served with naan bread, tandoori roti, or steamed basmati rice.

DAL — THE SOUL OF SOUTH ASIAN COOKING
Dal refers to dried lentils, peas, or beans, and also to the thick stew made from them. It is a staple food across India,
Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka. Common types include: Dal Tadka (yellow lentils tempered with mustard seeds,
cumin, garlic, and dried red chilies), Dal Makhani (black lentils slow-cooked with butter and cream), Chana Dal
(split chickpeas cooked with tomatoes and spices), Masoor Dal (red lentils, quick-cooking and nutritious), and
Moong Dal (split mung beans, light and easy to digest). The tempering (tadka/chaunk) process involves heating oil or ghee
and frying spices to release their essential oils before adding to the cooked lentils.
Dal is rich in protein, fiber, and essential nutrients, making it a critical protein source in vegetarian South Asian diets.

TANDOORI COOKING TRADITION
The tandoor is a cylindrical clay oven used extensively in South Asian cooking, reaching temperatures of 480°C (900°F).
Foods cooked in a tandoor include: Tandoori Chicken (marinated in yogurt, lemon juice, and spices like paprika, cumin,
garam masala, cooked at high heat), Naan bread (leavened flatbread that sticks to the inner walls of the tandoor),
Seekh Kebab (minced meat on skewers), Tandoori Fish, and Paneer Tikka (marinated cottage cheese).
The tandoor imparts a unique smoky, charred flavor impossible to replicate with conventional ovens.
Tandoori cuisine originated in the Punjab region and spread globally through Punjabi restaurants.
For home cooking, a cast-iron grill or broiler can partially replicate tandoor results.

SOUTH ASIAN SPICE GUIDE — ESSENTIAL SPICES
Turmeric (Haldi): Golden-yellow spice with earthy flavor, anti-inflammatory properties, used in almost every curry.
Cumin (Jeera): Warm, earthy spice used whole or ground, essential for tempering and spice blends.
Coriander (Dhania): Seeds and leaves both used; seeds are citrusy and nutty; fresh leaves add brightness.
Cardamom (Elaichi): Green cardamom for sweet dishes and tea; black cardamom for savory dishes and biryani.
Garam Masala: A blend of warming spices (cinnamon, cardamom, cloves, black pepper, nutmeg) added at the end of cooking.
Fenugreek (Methi): Slightly bitter leaves and seeds used in curries, breads (methi paratha), and spice mixes.
Mustard Seeds (Rai/Sarson): Used for tempering in South Indian cuisine, popping in hot oil before adding other ingredients.
Asafoetida (Hing): Pungent resin used in tiny amounts; essential in lentil dishes and Indian pickles.
Kashmiri Red Chili: Provides vibrant red color with mild heat, used in tandoori marinades and Rogan Josh.

COOKING TECHNIQUES IN SOUTH ASIAN CUISINE
Dum Cooking: Slow cooking in a sealed vessel trapping steam; used for biryani, dum aloo, and slow-cooked meats.
Tempering (Tadka/Tarka/Chaunk): Frying whole spices in hot oil/ghee to release flavor; poured over dal, chutneys.
Bhuna: Dry-frying technique where meat or vegetables are cooked on high heat, constantly stirred until moisture evaporates.
Tandoor Roasting: Clay oven cooking at extreme heat for breads and marinated meats.
Korma: Braising meat or vegetables in yogurt-based sauce with nuts; a royal Mughal cooking technique.
Pasanda: Tenderized, flattened meat cooked in a rich almond-cream sauce.
Dhungar: Smoking technique using hot charcoal and ghee placed in a bowl within the dish to impart smoky flavor.
Kofta: Minced meat or vegetable balls cooked in curry; technique involves binding with spices and chickpea flour.
